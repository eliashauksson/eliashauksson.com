My journey into engineering started early. At around twelve, I began coding my own games in Python and Pygame - simple projects that showed me how ideas could turn into something real and interactive.

When I began my bachelor's in Electrical Engineering at EPFL, that curiosity naturally expanded into hardware. I discovered a passion for designing and building systems that bridge software and electronics. Alongside my coursework, I joined the EPFL Rocket Team, where I worked on PCB design and embedded programming for various subsystems.

For my bachelor thesis, I joined EPFLoop, the Hyperloop team of EPFL, contributing to the next-generation motherboard of the pod. I focused on renewing and improving PCBs, enhancing functionality for sensor routing and high-voltage, high-current distribution.

Beyond university, I spent a summer at Hauksson Weine, developing a TensorFlow-based AI model to help a rover navigate autonomously through a vineyard. Later, I worked for a year at SkyWatch A/S, as part of the hardware team, where I developed hardware testing tools and wrote a Python library for motor controller communication.

Outside of engineering, I spend most of my free time in the mountains. I'm drawn to endurance sports - especially trail running and mountaineering - where focus and persistence matter as much as strength.

I've completed several ultra trail races, including the Eiger Ultra Trail E51 (51km, 3000m D+) and the Laugavegur Ultra Marathon in Iceland (55k, 1800m D+). These experiences taught me how to manage effort, discomfort, and the long-term mindset needed to reach the finish line. Looking ahead, I plan to take on longer challenges such as the Eiger Ultra E101, and one day, maybe even the infamous UTMB 100 miler.

Beyond running, I love mountaineering and rock climbing, spending time on alpine routes and learning to move more confidently on technical terrain. My goal is to eventually take on some of the classic ascents of the Alps - peaks like the Eiger, Mönch, Jungfrau, or Mont Blanc.

In the past years, I've also grown to enjoy cycling, both for training and travel. I took part in my first road race, the Tour de Léman (180km), and I'm now preparing for a bikepacking journey from Portugal to Nordkapp, Norway - a multi-month trip I plan to begin after completing my military service.

In summer 2025, I began my military service at the Rettungsschule 75 as a Rettungssoldat (rescue soldier). Our work focused on technical rescue operations: Setting up long-distance water transports for firefighting, clearing debris, cutting through concrete and steel, and safely rescuing injured people from collapsed structures. We also trained in first aid and tactical response, learning to stay calm and make decisions under pressure.

Beyond the technical skills, military life taught me a lot about teamwork, endurance, and discipline: How to perform when you're cold, tired, or under stress, and still keep people safe.

I have since completed the Unteroffizierschule (Non-Commissioned Officer School), where I trained to lead small teams effectively in demanding environments, eventually earning the rank of Wachtmeister (Sergeant). I am now returning for my second service period in this role.

Looking ahead, I plan to continue to the Offizierschule (Officer School) to become a Leutnant (Lieutenant). This next step focuses on taking responsibility for larger groups, coordinating operations, and developing a structured and confident leadership style.

For me, engineering, endurance sports, and military service all connect through the same basic idea: building the composure to solve real problems when conditions are imperfect. Whether I am designing electronics, moving through the mountains, or leading a small team under pressure, I care about reliability, clarity, and thoughtful decisions that hold up in the real world.
